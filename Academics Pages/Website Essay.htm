Is Power Corruptive?

What is power? Power is when one or more individuals are in a state that is superior to that of other individuals. Some use power to garner a common voice and to help solve widespread problems. However, there are also those who use power to pursue their own nefarious purposes. These types of people don’t care about the greater good and only think about themselves, although they may say that they care. The denizens who are inferior to and who are impacted by these selfish people, as well as those who are witnesses to this exploitation of power, would all agree with the statement, “Power, by it’s nature, is corruptive.” This statement is addressed and proven in Ken Kesey’s One Flew Over the Cuckoo’s Nest.

One Flew Over the Cuckoo’s Nest shows the way that power is corruptive by depicting the presence of fear in the psychiatric ward where a group of mental patients live. A really oppressive nurse, known as the Big Nurse, monitors this group. The Big Nurse is also in charge of everything that goes on in the facility. She is a prime example of someone who has a lot of power but takes advantage of it. She does this by using her power to exploit the patients’ problems and fears while traumatizing them, ultimately to run the ward in the way she sees fit, a way that includes her staying in power. One example of this is stated in “Actually, there isn’t much need for them…that side.” (17) This paragraph shows how the Big Nurse uses her power by showing that she scares the patients (in this case the Acutes, who are somewhat mentally crippled) into cooperating with her. She scares them by suggesting that if they don’t cooperate, then they will end up on the side of the ward with the Chronics, patients who have even more mental problems.

The Big Nurse also keeps everyone in check by humiliating patients for the purpose that this humiliation will hinder any thoughts of defiance. This is shown in “The group…talking about.” (50-54) These pages discuss a group meeting that the patients and the staff of the hospital had together in the preceding pages. These group meetings are times when one of the staff members, typically the Big Nurse, discusses a particular patient’s problem and suggests a solution. There are numerous problems with the way these group meetings work, and these problems reveal the Big Nurse’s corruption. In these group meetings, all the patients gather together, making them have to sit through a discussion of one patient’s problem. The patient whose problem is being discussed suffers a rapid decrease in self-esteem, since he is the only one being targeted. In these pages from the book, after the group meeting ends, one of the patients, McMurphy, speaks to the patient who was targeted, Harding, about what the group meeting was like. According to McMurphy, they are like “pecking parties.” In these “pecking parties,” every patient is like a chicken, and each chicken is pecking at a particular chicken’s wound. Based on these pages, it can be concluded that McMurphy is an example of a person who believes that power is corruptive. At first, Harding disagrees with McMurphy, stating that the Big Nurse is only trying to help the patients develop a strong character by making them learn from their mistakes. As he thinks about it though, he realizes that McMurphy is right, and that the Big Nurse is oppressing them.

Aside from planting fear in the patients and humiliating them, the Big Nurse also uses power corruptly by traumatizing patients, that too, in front of other patients. This happens at the end of Part IV, at the part where McMurphy was to escape from the ward. It is shown in, “Billy wasn’t awake to… throat cut.” (270-274) The night before this scene, there was a small secret get-together in the ward, with only McMurphy, Chief, Billy Bibbit, McMurphy’s friends Candy and Sandy, and other characters like Mr. Turkle in attendance. The main purpose of the gathering was to get McMurphy to escape from the ward, because there was a feeling that McMurphy couldn’t win against the Big Nurse and her tyrannical character. They make a plan on what to do, and this plan would work if everyone wakes up early the next day. However, the next day, no one wakes up early, which messes up everything. This failure gives the Big Nurse another chance to use her power, which started after she finds Billy Bibbit sleeping with one of McMurphy’s friends. Because Billy loves his mother and can’t stand seeing her disappointed at him, the Big Nurse uses this to her advantage and tells Billy that she will be discussing this incident with Billy’s mother. This really breaks Billy down mentally, if he wasn’t already broken down. Even after much of his begging, the Big Nurse doesn’t change her decision. After Billy knows that it is all over for him, he cuts his throat, ending his suffering. This shows how the Big Nurse traumatized a patient in order to stay in power. If the Big Nurse hadn’t threatened to tell Billy’s mother about the incident, then it would have made her look like she showed remorse and pity, which she didn’t want.

Even after all this evidence that the Big Nurse is an example of corrupt power, some may still argue that she is only doing this for the well-being of the patients, so that when they move on with life, they can live in an orderly fashion. However, they fail to realize that after all this unleashing of power, no patient has become any better than he originally was. They also don’t realize the true motive of the Big Nurse’s enforcing of rules, which is to be superior to others.

In conclusion, this is why I agree that “Power, by its nature, is corruptive.” The evidence that supports this statement is shown in Ken Kesey’s One Flew Over the Cuckoo’s Nest. The way that corrupt power is shown in the book is also shown in our own lives, as we witness those who claim that they are just doing good, but in actuality are only concerned about themselves.
